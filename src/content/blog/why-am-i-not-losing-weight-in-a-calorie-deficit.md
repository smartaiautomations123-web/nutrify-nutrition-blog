If you keep asking yourself *why am I not losing weight in a calorie deficit*, take a breath — you're almost certainly not broken, and the laws of physics haven't switched off just for you. In nearly every case, one of five things is going on, and most of them are fixable once you can see them clearly.

A calorie deficit still works. What usually happens is that the deficit isn't as big as you think, or fat loss *is* happening but something else on the scale is hiding it. Let's go through the five most common culprits, with a practical fix for each.

## 1. You're probably not in the deficit you think you are

This is the big one, and it's nobody's fault — it's just how hard accurate tracking is. Study after study shows that people **under-report what they eat** by 20–50%, and the people who under-report most are often the ones most frustrated by stalled weight loss. You're not lying; you simply can't see the gaps. Here's where they hide:

- **Cooking oils and fats.** A "splash" of olive oil is easily 2–3 tablespoons — that's **240–360 calories** before you've added anything. Butter, mayo, dressings and cooking sprays used with a heavy hand add up fast.
- **Liquid calories.** A flat white, a glass of wine, a smoothie, juice, a "healthy" oat-milk latte. Drinks slip past mental tracking because they don't feel like food. A couple of glasses of wine and a latte can be 500+ calories a day.
- **Bites, licks and tastes (BLTs).** The crusts off your kid's toast, a spoonful while cooking, three chips from someone's plate. Untracked nibbles routinely add 100–300 calories.
- **Weekend drift.** Tracking Monday to Friday and "relaxing" Saturday and Sunday is the classic trap. Two loose days at +1,000 calories can wipe out five disciplined days of a 500-calorie deficit. The maths is brutal but real.
- **Portion creep.** Eyeballed portions quietly grow over weeks. The "100g of rice" you started with becomes 180g without you noticing.

**The fix:** log everything for two weeks — yes, the oil, the wine, the weekend, the bites. The goal isn't to do this forever; it's to recalibrate your eye. ## 2. Water retention is masking real fat loss

Here's the reassuring part: you might be losing fat *right now* and just not seeing it, because **body water swings far more than body fat day to day**. Fat loss is slow and steady — roughly half a kilo a week on a sensible deficit. Water can move two or three kilos in a single day. When water goes up while fat quietly comes down, the scale looks flat or even rises, and it feels like nothing is working.

Common reasons you're holding water:

- **Sodium and carbs.** A salty takeaway or a higher-carb day pulls water into your tissues. Every gram of stored carbohydrate (**glycogen**) holds roughly 3 grams of water with it.
- **New or harder training.** Start a new gym programme and your muscles retain water to repair micro-damage. This is a *good* sign that often masks fat loss for a week or two.
- **Stress and poor sleep.** Elevated **cortisol** makes you hold onto water. A stressful week can flatten the scale entirely.
- **Hormonal cycles.** For people who menstruate, water weight can rise several kilos in the days before a period, then drop sharply afterwards.

**The fix:** don't react to a single high morning. Keep training, keep your deficit, stay hydrated (drinking more water actually helps you hold *less*), and wait. The water you're holding from a new workout or a salty meal will release once your body settles — often as a sudden "whoosh" overnight.

## 3. Your TDEE is lower than the calculator told you

Online calculators give you an **estimate** of your Total Daily Energy Expenditure, not a guarantee. The real number can be 10–20% lower than predicted, especially if you're shorter, older, have dieted a lot before, or simply move less than the activity setting you picked. If your true maintenance is 1,900 calories but you've been eating to a calculated 2,100-calorie "deficit," you're actually at maintenance — and the scale agrees.

There's also **metabolic adaptation** (sometimes called adaptive thermogenesis). As you lose weight, a smaller body genuinely burns fewer calories, and your body also nudges down non-exercise movement — you fidget less, take the lift, feel a bit more tired. The deficit you set six weeks ago slowly shrinks toward zero without you changing a thing. This is normal and reversible, not a sign your metabolism is "damaged."

**The fix:** if two to three weeks of *accurate* logging show no trend-line movement, your maintenance is lower than you assumed. Trim intake by a modest 100–150 calories, or add some daily steps, rather than slashing food dramatically. Periodic **diet breaks** at maintenance can also ease adaptation and make the deficit more sustainable.

## 4. You're gaining muscle while losing fat

If you've recently started **resistance training** — especially if you're new to it, returning after a break, or eating plenty of protein — you can build muscle and lose fat at the same time. This is "body recomposition," and it's fantastic for how you look and feel, but it's terrible for your relationship with the scale, because muscle and fat roughly cancel out in weight even as your body visibly changes.

The classic clue: the scale is stuck, but your clothes are looser, your face looks leaner, and you're getting stronger. That's a win the scale simply can't measure.

**The fix:** stop letting one number define progress. Take monthly photos, measure your waist with a tape, and notice how your jeans fit. If those are all moving in the right direction while the scale holds, the plan is working — keep going.

## 5. You're measuring wrong (scale noise vs the real trend)

Daily weight is **noisy data**. It's swayed by what you ate yesterday, sodium, hydration, hormones, when you last used the loo, and even how well you slept. Weighing once a week and comparing two single days means you're often comparing two random points of noise — which can show a "gain" in the middle of genuine fat loss and convince you to quit.

**The fix:** weigh yourself at the same time each morning, after the toilet and before food, and track the **weekly average** rather than any single day. Compare this week's average to last week's. Over three to four weeks a real trend emerges from the noise — and that trend is the only number worth reacting to.

## A quick, kind note

This is general guidance, and individual needs genuinely vary. Plenty of normal things — medications, thyroid conditions, PCOS, perimenopause and others — affect weight and water retention. If your weight changes are unexplained, sudden, or you simply feel something's off, please check in with your GP or a registered dietitian. Getting personalised advice isn't a failure; it's smart.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should I stay in a deficit before worrying the scale is stuck?**
Give it three to four weeks of accurate tracking and weekly averages before changing anything. Shorter than that and you're mostly looking at water and daily noise, not fat.

**Can eating too little stop weight loss?**
Not in the "starvation mode" myth sense — you won't store fat from eating too little. But very low intake is hard to sustain, often leads to bigger untracked binges, and drives down your daily movement, which shrinks the real deficit. A moderate deficit you can actually stick to beats an aggressive one you can't.

**Why did I gain weight overnight even though I ate in a deficit?**
Almost always water, not fat. To gain half a kilo of actual fat overnight you'd need to eat about 3,500 calories *over* maintenance in one day. An overnight jump is sodium, carbs, hormones or a hard workout — it passes.

**Does when I eat matter if my calories are right?**
For fat loss, total calories over the week are what count, not the clock. Late eating gets blamed unfairly — the issue is usually the *extra* calories that come with late-night grazing, not the timing itself. There's more on this in [is it bad to eat late at night when trying to lose weight](/blog/is-it-bad-to-eat-late-at-night-when-trying-to-lose-weight/).

**What's the single most effective change if I'm stuck?**
Track honestly for two weeks — including oils, drinks, weekends and bites. Most stalls vanish the moment hidden calories become visible. Planning ahead helps too; some simple [weight loss meal prep for beginners](/blog/weight-loss-meal-prep-for-beginners/) removes the guesswork that lets calories creep in.

## The bottom line

If you're genuinely in a deficit, fat loss is happening — the question is whether your deficit is real and whether the scale is showing it through the noise. Tighten your tracking, judge progress by weekly trends and how your clothes fit, and give it a few weeks before changing course.
