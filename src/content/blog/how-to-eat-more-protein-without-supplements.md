If you've been wondering **how to eat more protein without supplements**, the good news is that you almost certainly don't need a tub of powder, a shaker bottle, or a single protein bar to do it. Real food does the job beautifully — chicken, eggs, fish, dairy, beans, lentils, tofu — and with a few simple habits you can comfortably hit a high daily protein target using nothing but what's already on your plate.

This guide walks through how much protein you actually need, the whole foods that deliver the most, the easy swaps and snack upgrades that add grams without effort, and a dead-simple framework for spreading protein across the day.

> **A quick note before we start:** This is general nutrition guidance, not personalised medical advice. Individual protein needs vary with your age, body size, activity level and health. If you have kidney concerns or any medical condition affecting how much protein you should eat, follow the personalised advice of your doctor or a registered dietitian.

## Step one: set a daily protein target

You can't aim at a number you haven't picked. Most adults under-eat protein simply because they've never decided what "enough" looks like for them.

Here are the general ranges nutrition professionals commonly reference:

- A widely cited range for healthy, active adults is roughly **1.2 to 2.0 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight** per day (about **0.5 to 0.9 grams per pound**).
- A sensible middle-ground target for most people is around **1.6 g/kg**.
- If you weigh 70 kg (about 11 stone), that's roughly **85 to 110 grams of protein a day**.

For a lot of people, the headline goal works out to somewhere around **100 grams a day** — a tidy, memorable number that's high enough to support muscle, satiety and recovery for an average adult. Treat these as starting points, not rules: someone very active or older may aim higher, and **individual needs vary**.

## Step two: anchor protein at every meal

Here's the single most useful habit in this whole guide. Instead of trying to cram your protein into one big dinner, **anchor a solid serving of protein at every eating occasion**. Three meals at 30 grams each gets you to 90 before you've touched a snack.

The reason this works isn't just arithmetic. Your body uses protein most efficiently when it's **spread across the day** rather than dumped in one sitting, and front-loading some at breakfast keeps you fuller and steadies your appetite for hours. Most people's weak spot is the morning — toast, cereal and fruit are nearly protein-free — so fixing breakfast alone can add 20–30 grams a day. (Our companion guide to [high-protein breakfast ideas](/blog/high-protein-breakfast-ideas/) is built entirely around this.)

The mental model: **every plate should answer the question "where's the protein?"** before anything else goes on it.

## Step three: know your highest-protein whole foods

You don't need to memorise a database — just keep a mental shortlist of dense, everyday options. Here are the heavy hitters you'll find in any UK supermarket, with rough protein per typical serving:

**Animal proteins**

- **Chicken breast** — about **30 g** per skinless 100 g cooked portion. The workhorse.
- **Turkey breast** — similar to chicken, lean and versatile.
- **Tinned tuna** — around **25 g** per drained 100 g tin. Cheap, fast, no cooking.
- **Salmon and white fish** — roughly **20–25 g** per fillet, plus omega-3s.
- **Eggs** — about **6 g** each, so three eggs is ~18 g of genuinely cheap protein.
- **Greek yogurt** (the thick, strained kind) — around **10 g** per 100 g; a big bowl easily clears 15–20 g.
- **Cottage cheese** — about **12–14 g** per half-pot. Underrated and very protein-dense.
- **Reduced-fat hard cheese** — around **7–8 g** per 30 g.
- **Lean mince (beef, turkey or pork) 5% fat** — roughly **25–28 g** per 100 g cooked.

**Plant proteins**

- **Tofu and tempeh** — about **8 g** and **19 g** per 100 g respectively.
- **Edamame** — around **11 g** per 100 g. A brilliant snack.
- **Lentils** — about **9 g** per 100 g cooked.
- **Chickpeas and other beans** — roughly **7–9 g** per 100 g cooked.
- **Whole grains like quinoa** — about **4–5 g** per 100 g cooked, plus what they add to a mixed plate.

If you want the headline answer to "what food is highest in protein?" — by weight, it's lean cooked meats, fish and prawns, sitting at roughly **25–30 g per 100 g**.

## Step four: make easy food swaps

You don't have to overhaul your diet. Most of the gains come from quietly upgrading foods you already eat:

- **Greek yogurt instead of regular yogurt** — doubles the protein for the same spoonful.
- **Greek yogurt instead of mayo** in tuna, chicken or egg salads — saves fat and adds a few grams.
- **Wholemeal or higher-protein bread** rather than white — small but free.
- **Skyr or quark instead of cream** stirred into sauces and porridge.
- **Pasta made part-lentil or part-chickpea** — bumps a typically low-protein meal up by 10+ grams.
- **A scattering of seeds or a spoon of nut butter** on porridge and yogurt.
- **Frozen edamame instead of peas** as your default green side.

None of these change what a meal *is* — they just make each one carry more protein.

## Step five: upgrade your snacks

Snacks are where most people leak the easiest protein wins, because the default options — crisps, biscuits, fruit, a flapjack — are nearly all carbohydrate. Swap in **protein-forward snacks** and you can add 20–40 grams a day almost passively:

- A **boiled egg or two** (6 g each).
- A **pot of Greek yogurt or cottage cheese** (10–20 g).
- A **handful of edamame or roasted chickpeas** (7–11 g).
- A **small tin of tuna or mackerel** on oatcakes.
- A **piece of cheese with an apple** (7–8 g).
- **Roasted soya nuts or a small handful of mixed nuts** (nuts are protein-light but useful alongside the rest).

The rule of thumb: aim for a snack that delivers **at least 10 grams** of protein, not just calories.

## Step six: batch-cook so protein is always ready

The reason people fall short isn't usually willpower — it's that the high-protein option wasn't *ready* when they were hungry, so the biscuits won. Solve that once a week:

- **Roast or poach a tray of chicken breasts** and shred them for salads, wraps and bowls.
- **Boil half a dozen eggs** and keep them in the fridge for instant snacks.
- **Cook a big pot of lentils, chickpeas or chilli** that reheats all week.
- **Pre-portion Greek yogurt** with toppings into jars for grab-and-go breakfasts.

When the protein is already cooked and waiting, hitting your target stops being a daily decision and becomes the path of least resistance.

## Plant-based? You can still hit your target without supplements

Vegetarians and vegans absolutely can reach a high protein intake from whole foods — it just takes a little more intention, because plant proteins are generally less concentrated. The keys:

- **Lean on the densest plant options**: tofu, tempeh, edamame, lentils, beans and chickpeas at every meal.
- **Add dairy or eggs if you're vegetarian** — Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, eggs and milk are protein powerhouses.
- **Combine sources across the day** (beans with grains, for example) so you cover the full range of amino acids — this happens naturally over a varied day, no need to obsess meal by meal.
- **Don't forget the small stuff**: seeds, nuts, soya milk and wholegrain bread all stack up.

A vegan day built on tofu scramble, a big lentil-and-grain bowl, an edamame snack and a bean chilli can comfortably clear 80–100 grams — all whole food, zero powder.

## A simple "protein per meal" framework

If you remember nothing else, remember this:

1. **Pick a daily number** (around 100 g is a solid default for many adults).
2. **Divide it across your meals** — roughly **30 g at breakfast, 30 g at lunch, 30 g at dinner**, plus a **10 g snack**.
3. **Build each plate protein-first** — decide the protein, then add the carbs and veg around it.
4. **Check you actually hit it**, because almost everyone overestimates.

## FAQ

**How can I get 100g of protein a day from food?**
Spread it across the day: roughly 30 g at each of three meals plus a 10 g snack. For example — Greek yogurt with seeds at breakfast (~25 g), a chicken or tuna salad at lunch (~30 g), salmon or lean mince with lentils at dinner (~35 g), and a boiled egg or pot of cottage cheese as a snack (~12 g). That's 100+ grams entirely from whole food.

**What food is highest in protein?**
By weight, lean cooked meats, fish and prawns top the list at around **25–30 g per 100 g**. Chicken and turkey breast, white fish and tinned tuna are the most protein-dense everyday options. Among plant foods, tempeh, tofu and edamame lead the way.

**Can vegetarians get enough protein without supplements?**
Yes. Vegetarians who include dairy and eggs have it easy — Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, eggs and milk are all excellent. Vegans need a bit more planning but can hit a high target with tofu, tempeh, edamame, lentils, beans and chickpeas featured at every meal, backed up by grains, nuts and seeds.

**Do I really need protein powder to build muscle?**
No. Protein powder is convenient, but it's just a fast, concentrated source — it has no special muscle-building magic over chicken, eggs, dairy or beans. If you can hit your daily target through food, you don't need supplements at all.

**How do I know if I'm actually eating enough protein?**
Most people overestimate, so the only reliable way is to check. Track your meals for a week — even loosely — and compare the total against your target. A photo-based tracker makes this quick, but a notebook works too. The point is to replace guessing with a number.

Eating more protein without supplements comes down to a handful of habits: set a target, anchor a real serving of protein at every meal, lean on the densest whole foods, swap and snack smarter, and batch-cook so the high-protein choice is always the easy one. Whole food can carry you to 100 grams a day and beyond — no powders required.
