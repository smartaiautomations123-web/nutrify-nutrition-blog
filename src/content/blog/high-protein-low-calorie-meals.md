If you want **high protein low calorie meals** that keep you full while you're eating less, this is the list to save — 14 specific UK ideas across breakfast, lunch and dinner, each with rough calories *and* protein per serving so you can see exactly what you're getting. It's the most reliable combination for fat loss that doesn't leave you ravenous by mid-afternoon.

The logic is simple: protein is the macronutrient that fills you up the most per calorie, so loading your plate with it lets you stay in a **calorie deficit** without white-knuckling your way through constant hunger.

## Why high protein plus low calorie works for fat loss

Weight loss ultimately comes down to a **calorie deficit** — eating slightly less than you burn. The hard part isn't the maths, it's sticking with it when you're hungry. That's where protein earns its place.

**Protein is the most filling macro.** Gram for gram, it triggers fullness hormones and blunts ghrelin (your hunger signal) far more than carbs or fat. So a 400-calorie meal built around chicken or fish keeps you satisfied much longer than a 400-calorie pastry. Same calories, very different afternoon.

**It costs you calories to digest.** Your body burns roughly 20–30% of protein's calories just processing it — the **thermic effect of food** — versus around 5–10% for carbs and fat. It's a modest edge, but it's free.

**It protects your muscle.** When you lose weight, some of that loss can come from muscle rather than fat. Eating enough protein — and ideally doing some resistance training — tells your body to hold onto lean tissue and burn fat instead. That keeps your metabolism humming and your body looking toned rather than just smaller.

A sensible daily target for most people losing weight is around **1.6 to 2.2g of protein per kilo of body weight**. The meals below are built to help you hit that while keeping calories in check — most land between **300 and 500 calories** with **30g+ of protein**.

## High protein low calorie breakfasts

- **0% Greek yoghurt with berries and a spoon of seeds** — a 200g pot, a handful of raspberries and a sprinkle of mixed seeds comes to roughly **180 calories and 22g protein**. Add half a scoop of whey to push protein past 30g for barely any extra calories.
- **Three-egg veggie omelette** — three eggs with spinach, tomato and a little onion lands around **260 calories and 19g protein**, and it's endlessly adaptable to whatever's in the fridge.
- **Cottage cheese on a slice of rye with tomato** — half a tub of cottage cheese over seeded rye gives about **210 calories and 18g protein**, and it takes two minutes.
- **Overnight oats with skimmed milk and whey** — 40g oats soaked in skimmed milk with half a scoop of protein powder comes to roughly **300 calories and 28g protein**, ready before you've woken up.

## High protein low calorie lunches

- **Chicken and salad wrap** — a wholemeal wrap with 100g shredded chicken breast, salad and a smear of light mayo is about **350 calories and 32g protein**. Portable and genuinely filling.
- **Tuna and white bean salad** — a tin of tuna in spring water mixed with half a tin of cannellini beans, rocket and lemon delivers roughly **320 calories and 38g protein**, with fibre to spare.
- **Lentil and feta salad** — a pouch of pre-cooked Puy lentils with cherry tomatoes, cucumber and 30g feta sits around **380 calories and 21g protein** — an easy higher-protein vegetarian option.
- **Prawn and edamame rice bowl** — 120g cooked prawns, a handful of steamed edamame and a small portion of rice comes to about **420 calories and 34g protein**.
- **Leftover roast chicken with a big slaw** — 120g chicken breast and a vinegar-dressed slaw (no creamy mayo) lands near **330 calories and 36g protein**, and uses up Sunday's leftovers.

## High protein low calorie dinners

- **Grilled salmon with greens and new potatoes** — a 130g salmon fillet, a pile of broccoli and a few new potatoes is about **450 calories and 32g protein**, plus a hit of omega-3.
- **Turkey mince chilli** — 150g of 5%-fat turkey mince with kidney beans, chopped tomatoes and spices comes to roughly **400 calories and 38g protein** per bowl, and it freezes brilliantly.
- **Cod and chickpea traybake** — a cod loin roasted with a tin of chickpeas, peppers and paprika delivers around **380 calories and 35g protein** from one tray.
- **Tofu and vegetable stir-fry** — 200g firm tofu with mixed veg and a light soy-ginger sauce over a small nest of noodles sits near **430 calories and 24g protein** for a plant-based dinner.
- **Steak strips with roasted veg** — 120g lean rump steak sliced over roasted courgette, pepper and red onion comes to about **360 calories and 34g protein**, no carbs required to feel satisfied.

## Build-your-own formulas

Once you spot the pattern, you don't need a recipe at all. Every one of the meals above follows one of these simple templates:

- **Lean protein + non-starchy veg + a small smart carb.** A palm-sized portion of chicken, fish, tofu or lean mince; as much low-calorie veg as you like; and a modest portion of potato, rice or wholegrain. This is the everyday plate, and it almost always lands around 400 calories with 30g+ protein.
- **Protein + protein.** Stack two protein sources — beans with tuna, yoghurt with whey, eggs with smoked salmon — to push protein high while calories stay low.
- **Big volume, low energy density.** Bulk meals out with high-water, high-fibre foods (salad leaves, courgette, peppers, berries) so the plate looks generous for very few calories. **Volume eating** is the quiet trick behind feeling full in a deficit.

## Frequently asked questions

**What counts as a high protein, low calorie meal?**
As a rough rule, aim for a meal under about **500 calories** that delivers at least **25–30g of protein** — so roughly a gram of protein for every 15 calories or fewer. Lean meat, fish, eggs, dairy and pulses make this easy to hit.

**How much protein do I need to lose weight?**
Most people losing weight do well on **1.6 to 2.2g per kilo of body weight per day**. For a 70kg person that's around 110–155g, spread across three or four meals rather than dumped into one.

**Can I lose weight eating high protein meals?**
Protein doesn't melt fat on its own — weight loss still requires a **calorie deficit**. But because protein keeps you fuller for longer and protects muscle, it makes that deficit far easier to sustain, which is why higher-protein diets tend to work better in practice.

**Are these meals suitable for vegetarians?**
Several are — the lentil and feta salad, tofu stir-fry, Greek yoghurt breakfast and cottage cheese options all qualify. Pulses, tofu, eggs and dairy let you hit high protein without meat; you just need slightly larger portions to match the protein in fish or chicken.

**Do I need to count calories forever?**
No. Counting for a few weeks teaches you what a balanced, protein-rich plate looks like, and most people then eyeball it well. Snapping the occasional photo to spot-check keeps you honest without the chore of logging everything.
