If you've started tirzepatide and found yourself wincing at the occasional rotten-egg burp, **Mounjaro and sulfur burps** are a well-known pairing — unpleasant, a bit embarrassing, but rarely anything sinister. They tend to show up in the first weeks and after dose increases, and for most people they're tied directly to *what* you eat and *how* the medication slows your stomach down. This guide explains why they happen, which foods and habits set them off, and the practical tweaks that actually reduce them.

> **A quick note before we start:** This is general information, not medical advice. Mounjaro (tirzepatide) is a prescription medication, and any concerns about side effects should be discussed with your doctor or pharmacist, who know your full history. There's no dosing advice here, and nothing here should prompt you to change or stop your medication. **Get medical advice promptly** if your sulfur burps are severe or persistent, or if they come alongside significant abdominal pain, repeated vomiting, fever, or signs of dehydration — those warrant a proper check rather than a dietary tweak.

## What are sulfur burps, exactly?

A **sulfur burp** is a burp that smells like rotten eggs. That distinctive whiff comes from **hydrogen sulfide gas**, which is produced when bacteria in your gut break down sulfur-containing compounds in the food you've eaten. A normal burp is mostly swallowed air and is odourless; a sulfur burp means something has been sitting around long enough to ferment and release that gas, which then travels back up.

So the rotten-egg smell isn't a sign that anything is broken — it's the by-product of **fermentation**. The two questions that matter are simply: why is food fermenting more than usual, and where is the sulfur coming from?

## Why Mounjaro causes sulfur burps

The main culprit is **slowed gastric emptying**. Mounjaro is a GLP-1 and GIP receptor agonist, and one of the ways it curbs appetite is by keeping food in your stomach for much longer than normal. That delay is the whole point — it's what makes you feel full on less — but it has a side effect. When a meal sits in your stomach and upper gut for hours instead of moving on promptly, **bacteria have far more time to act on it**. Foods rich in sulfur ferment, hydrogen sulfide builds up, and the result rises back up as that telltale rotten-egg burp.

Two things make this worse early on. First, your digestion is adjusting to a genuinely new pace, so transit is at its slowest and most unpredictable. Second, **larger or higher-fat meals** sit even longer — fat is slow to leave the stomach at the best of times, and on tirzepatide that's amplified. Put a big, greasy, sulfur-heavy meal into a stomach that's already emptying in slow motion, and you've created close to ideal conditions for sulfur burps.

It's also why they tend to ease over time. As your body settles into the medication and you adapt your meals, the fermentation that drives the smell usually settles too.

## Foods and habits that trigger sulfur burps

Because the smell comes from sulfur-rich food fermenting in a slow gut, the triggers fall into two buckets: **what** you eat and **how** you eat it.

### Sulfur-rich foods (the usual suspects)

These are naturally high in sulfur compounds, so they give gut bacteria the raw material to produce hydrogen sulfide. They're healthy foods for most people — the point isn't to ban them, but to notice if *you* react:

- **Eggs** — one of the most common triggers, and the most literal source of that egg smell.
- **Cruciferous vegetables** — broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, Brussels sprouts, and kale.
- **Garlic and onions** (and leeks, shallots, spring onions).
- **Red meat**, which is high in sulfur-containing amino acids and slow to digest.
- **Dairy**, especially if you're a bit lactose-sensitive.
- Some **dried fruit and processed foods** preserved with sulphites.

### Habits that pile on

- **Large meals.** The bigger the meal, the longer it lingers and ferments. Smaller, more frequent portions clear faster.
- **High-fat, greasy food.** Fat slows stomach emptying the most, giving fermentation extra time.
- **Fizzy drinks.** Carbonation adds gas directly and encourages burping in the first place.
- **Eating quickly or talking while eating.** This swallows extra air, which has to come back up.
- **Lying down soon after eating.** Gravity normally helps keep things moving downward; reclining works against you.

The honest answer is that triggers are individual. Eggs might wreck one person's afternoon and do nothing to the next. The aim is to find *your* pattern rather than cut out a long list of nutritious foods you don't need to.

## How to reduce and prevent sulfur burps on Mounjaro

Once you understand the mechanism — sulfur-rich food fermenting in a slow stomach — the fixes are intuitive.

### Eat smaller, more frequent meals

A smaller meal clears your stomach faster, leaving less time for fermentation. On a medication that's already slowing things down, **portion size is one of the most powerful levers you have**. Splitting your intake into smaller meals across the day tends to cause far fewer burps than one or two large sittings.

### Go easier on the heaviest triggers

You don't need to eliminate eggs, garlic, or broccoli — but if you've noticed a clear link, **dialling back the portion or shifting it to a meal when symptoms bother you less** can make a real difference. Pay particular attention to **high-fat, greasy meals**, which combine the worst of both worlds: lots of sulfur potential *and* the slowest possible emptying.

### Slow down and stay upright

Eat **slowly and chew thoroughly** — it swallows less air and starts digestion properly. Then **stay upright for a couple of hours after eating** rather than slumping on the sofa or going to bed; letting gravity help your stomach empty cuts down on what comes back up.

### Cut the fizz and watch the fluids

Swap **fizzy drinks** for still water, which adds no extra gas. Staying generally well hydrated also helps keep digestion ticking over, though sip steadily rather than gulping large volumes with meals.

### Try simple over-the-counter options — after a quick check

Some people find **peppermint tea, ginger, or an over-the-counter remedy** eases the wind and the smell. Before reaching for any product, have a quick word with your pharmacist — they can confirm what's suitable alongside Mounjaro and your other medicines.

## FAQ

**Does Mounjaro cause sulfur burps?**
Yes, sulfur (rotten-egg) burps are a commonly reported side effect. They happen because tirzepatide slows gastric emptying, so food — especially sulfur-rich and high-fat food — sits in your stomach longer and ferments, producing hydrogen sulfide gas. They're often most noticeable early on and after dose increases, and tend to ease as your body adjusts.

**What do sulfur burps smell like, and is it normal?**
They smell like rotten eggs, which is the hydrogen sulfide gas released when sulfur-containing food ferments in the gut. An occasional sulfur burp on Mounjaro is common and usually harmless. What's *not* routine is severe or persistent burping alongside significant pain, repeated vomiting, or fever — that's worth getting checked.

**Which foods trigger sulfur burps on Mounjaro?**
The main offenders are sulfur-rich foods: eggs, cruciferous veg (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, sprouts), garlic, onions, red meat, and dairy. Large meals, greasy high-fat food, and fizzy drinks make them worse by slowing digestion or adding gas. Triggers are individual, so it's worth tracking which foods actually affect you rather than cutting everything.

**How do I get rid of sulfur burps fast?**
In the moment, sipping still water, peppermint or ginger tea, and staying upright can help move things along. To prevent them, eat smaller and slower meals, ease off your personal trigger foods and greasy dishes, skip fizzy drinks, and don't lie down straight after eating. Ask your pharmacist before trying any over-the-counter remedy.

**How long do sulfur burps on Mounjaro last?**
For most people they're worst in the first few weeks and after each dose increase, then settle as the body adapts and meals are adjusted. If they're persistent, severe, or come with other symptoms like pain or vomiting, speak to your doctor or pharmacist rather than waiting it out.

## The bottom line

The link between **Mounjaro and sulfur burps** comes down to one thing: a slowed stomach gives sulfur-rich food extra time to ferment, releasing the rotten-egg gas you're tasting. So the fixes follow naturally — eat smaller, less greasy meals, ease off your personal trigger foods, skip the fizz, and stay upright after eating, checking with your pharmacist before any remedy.
