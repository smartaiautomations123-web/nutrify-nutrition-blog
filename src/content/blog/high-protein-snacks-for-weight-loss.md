If you're after **high protein snacks for weight loss**, the goal is simple: snacks that deliver a decent hit of protein, stay reasonably low in calories, and actually keep you full until your next meal. Get that combination right and snacking stops being the thing that quietly derails your week — it becomes one of the easiest ways to stay in a calorie deficit without feeling deprived.

Most British snacking defaults — a packet of crisps, a couple of biscuits, a cereal bar — are the opposite of that: plenty of calories, barely any protein, and you're hungry again within the hour. Swapping a few of those for the options below changes how the gaps between meals feel.

## Why protein snacks help with weight loss

Protein earns its place in a weight-loss snack for three evidence-backed reasons.

**It keeps you fuller for longer.** Protein is the most **satiating** of the three macronutrients. It nudges up fullness hormones like GLP-1 and PYY while blunting ghrelin, the hormone that drives hunger. A protein-led snack tends to genuinely tide you over, rather than leaving you rummaging for something else 40 minutes later.

**It protects muscle in a calorie deficit.** When you're eating less to lose fat, your body can dip into muscle as well as fat unless you give it enough protein. Spreading your intake across the day — including snacks — helps support **muscle protein synthesis**, which keeps your metabolism ticking and means more of the weight you lose is fat rather than muscle.

**It cuts cravings.** Because protein steadies appetite and blunts the blood-sugar spike-and-crash you get from sugary or refined-carb snacks, people who snack on protein typically report fewer cravings and end up eating less overall — without consciously trying to.

The sweet spot for a weight-loss snack is roughly **10 to 20 grams of protein for under 200 calories**. The ideas below are grouped so you can find one to suit whatever you've got time for.

## Shop-bought and grab-and-go

For when you need something fast with zero prep.

- **A pot of 0% Greek yoghurt** — a 170g pot lands around **17g protein for ~95 calories**. The single best-value snack in any UK supermarket.
- **Skyr pot** — Icelandic-style and even denser; a 150g pot gives roughly **15g for ~90 calories**.
- **Light Babybel or a couple of cheese portions** — three Babybel Light bring about **15g for ~125 calories** and travel anywhere.
- **A protein bar** — pick one with 15g+ protein and under 200 calories (Grenade, Barebells and Trek do solid versions); typically **15–20g for ~190 calories**. Check the sugar.
- **Beef or turkey jerky / biltong** — a 25g pack of biltong is roughly **15g for ~80 calories**, all protein and very little else.
- **A ready-to-drink protein shake** — UFIT, Grenade or own-brand cartons run **20–25g for ~150 calories**, ideal when you're out.
- **Edamame pods** — a 100g portion (sold ready-steamed in most supermarkets) gives about **11g for ~120 calories**, plus fibre.

## Savoury homemade

A couple of minutes of assembly, big payoff on fullness.

- **Cottage cheese with cherry tomatoes** — half a tub (around 150g) is roughly **14g for ~120 calories**; black pepper and a few tomatoes make it a proper snack.
- **Tuna on a couple of oatcakes** — half a tin of tuna in spring water on two oatcakes lands about **18g for ~180 calories**.
- **Boiled eggs** — two eggs are around **13g for ~140 calories**, endlessly portable and prepped in one go.
- **Roasted chickpeas** — a 100g portion roasted with paprika comes to roughly **9g for ~160 calories** with a satisfying crunch and a hit of fibre.
- **Ham or turkey slices rolled around a cheese stick** — three slices with a light cheese stick is about **16g for ~140 calories**.

## Sweet homemade

For when you want something that feels like a treat but pulls its weight.

- **Greek yoghurt with berries and a few seeds** — 150g yoghurt, a handful of berries and a teaspoon of seeds gives around **16g for ~160 calories**.
- **Chocolate protein mousse** — Greek yoghurt blended with a scoop of chocolate protein powder makes a thick mousse at roughly **30g for ~200 calories** — closer to dessert than diet food.
- **A small apple with a tablespoon of peanut butter** — about **5g protein for ~180 calories**; the apple's fibre stretches the satiety further (a lighter protein hit, but a genuinely filling combo).
- **Cottage cheese with a drizzle of honey and cinnamon** — half a tub sweetened lands around **14g for ~150 calories**.

## High-protein, low-calorie heroes

When the priority is maximum protein for the fewest possible calories — the ones to lean on hardest in a deficit.

- **Prawns with a squeeze of lemon** — 100g cooked prawns are about **18g for just ~80 calories**, one of the leanest protein hits going.
- **Quark with sweetener and vanilla** — a 100g portion of this fat-free soft cheese is roughly **12g for ~70 calories** and makes a brilliant yoghurt swap.
- **Smoked salmon, a slice or two** — around **9g for ~70 calories** per couple of slices, no cooking needed.
- **Egg-white omelette or a few egg whites** — three egg whites scramble up to about **11g for ~50 calories**, the closest thing to pure protein you can put on a plate.

## Frequently asked questions

**What's the best high-protein snack for weight loss?**
For sheer value, it's hard to beat a pot of **0% Greek yoghurt** — around 17g of protein for under 100 calories, no prep, available everywhere. If you want the absolute leanest protein-to-calorie ratio, prawns, quark and egg whites are the standouts.

**How much protein should a weight-loss snack have?**
Aim for roughly **10 to 20 grams of protein for under 200 calories**. That's enough to meaningfully blunt hunger and support muscle while keeping the snack comfortably within a calorie deficit.

**Are protein bars good for losing weight?**
They can be, in moderation. The best ones pack 15g+ of protein for under 200 calories and are genuinely handy when you're out. The catch is that some are closer to chocolate bars in disguise — check the sugar and calories, and treat them as a convenient backup rather than a daily default.

**Can high-protein snacks actually help me eat less?**
Yes — that's largely the point. By keeping you fuller for longer and softening cravings, protein snacks tend to reduce how much you eat at later meals and across the day. They're a supporting tactic within an overall calorie deficit, not a magic fix on their own.

**What can I snack on at night that won't ruin my progress?**
Slow-digesting, low-calorie protein works well in the evening: a small bowl of quark or cottage cheese, a chocolate protein mousse made with Greek yoghurt, or a few egg whites all satisfy a late craving for around 70–200 calories without piling on the numbers.
