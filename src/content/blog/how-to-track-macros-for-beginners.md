Learning **how to track macros for beginners** is mostly about getting comfortable with a few simple numbers and then building a habit you can actually stick to. Macros sound technical, but they're just the three nutrients that make up everything you eat — and once you understand them, tracking becomes a quick daily routine rather than a maths exam.

This is the complete beginner's guide: what macros are, how to set targets for your goal, the real-world ways to track them, how to read a label without squinting, and the mistakes that trip most people up in week one.

## What are macros (and why bother)?

**Macros** — short for **macronutrients** — are the three nutrients your body needs in large amounts: **protein**, **carbohydrates**, and **fat**. They're the only things in food that provide energy, which we measure in **calories**.

Each macro carries a fixed amount of energy:

- **Protein** — 4 calories per gram. Builds and repairs muscle, and it's the most **satiating** macro, so it keeps you full.
- **Carbohydrates** — 4 calories per gram. Your body's main fuel source, especially for exercise and brain function.
- **Fat** — 9 calories per gram. Essential for hormones, vitamin absorption, and cell health. It's calorie-dense, which is why portions matter.

Here's the key point that makes tracking worthwhile: **your total calories determine whether you gain, lose, or maintain weight, but your macro split determines body composition and how you feel.** Two people eating 2,000 calories a day can have wildly different results depending on whether they're getting 80g of protein or 200g. Tracking macros — rather than just calories — gives you that extra layer of control.

You don't need to track forever. Most beginners use it as a **learning tool** for a few weeks or months to understand portion sizes and where their food actually comes from, then keep the habits and drop the logging.

## How to set your macro and calorie targets

You can't track toward a goal you haven't set. Here's the beginner-friendly version of the maths.

**Step 1 — Estimate your calorie needs.** Start with your **maintenance calories** (the amount that keeps your weight stable). A rough starting point for many adults is bodyweight in kilograms × 30–33 for a moderately active person, but online calculators that factor in your age, height, sex, and activity level will get you closer. To lose weight, you'll want a moderate [calorie deficit](/blog/what-is-a-calorie-deficit/) — typically 300–500 calories below maintenance.

**Step 2 — Set protein first.** Protein is the macro that matters most for body composition. A practical target is **1.6 to 2.2g of protein per kg of bodyweight** if you're active or trying to lose fat while keeping muscle. For a 70kg person, that's roughly 112–154g a day.

**Step 3 — Set fat next.** Aim for around **0.6 to 1g per kg of bodyweight** to support hormones and general health. For that same 70kg person, that's about 42–70g.

**Step 4 — Fill the rest with carbs.** Whatever calories are left after protein and fat go to carbohydrates. Carbs are the most flexible macro — they fuel training and make food enjoyable, so don't fear them.

A worked example for a 70kg person aiming to lose fat at 1,900 calories a day might land near **140g protein, 60g fat, and 190g carbs**. These are starting points, not laws — adjust based on how your body and energy respond over a few weeks.

## The actual methods for tracking macros

This is where beginners get stuck, so here are the real options, from most precise to most forgiving.

**1. A tracking app.** The standard approach. You log what you eat and the app totals your macros against your targets using a food database. This is by far the easiest way to start, because you're not doing arithmetic — the app handles it.

**2. Weighing food with a kitchen scale.** The most accurate method. You weigh portions in grams and log the exact amount. It feels fiddly at first but takes seconds once it's a habit, and it quickly retrains your eye for what a real portion looks like. You don't have to weigh everything forever — many people weigh for a few weeks, then estimate.

**3. Estimating with portions.** The hand or eyeball method — a palm of protein, a cupped hand of carbs, a thumb of fat. Less precise, but genuinely useful when you're out, travelling, or just don't want the friction. We go deep on this in [how to track calories without weighing food](/blog/how-to-track-calories-without-weighing-food/).

**4. Photo logging.** Newer apps let you snap a photo of your plate and have the food recognised and logged automatically. This removes the single biggest barrier for beginners — the manual searching and data entry that makes people quit by week two. With [Nutrify](https://nutrify.info), you photograph your meal and it estimates the calories, protein, and macros for you, so the habit survives the busy days when you'd otherwise skip logging entirely.

Most people end up blending these: weigh at home, estimate or photo-log when out. The best method is simply **the one you'll keep doing**.

## How to read a nutrition label

UK and EU labels make this straightforward once you know where to look.

- **Find the "per 100g" column first.** It lets you compare products fairly, regardless of pack size. Use the "per serving" column for what you're actually eating — but check whether the stated serving matches your real portion (it often doesn't).
- **Spot your three macros.** Labels list **Fat**, **Carbohydrate**, and **Protein** in grams. "Of which sugars" and "of which saturates" sit underneath as subcategories — they're already included in the totals, so don't double-count them.
- **Sanity-check the calories.** The energy value is shown in both **kJ and kcal** — kcal is the one you're tracking. You can roughly verify it: (protein g × 4) + (carbs g × 4) + (fat g × 9) should land near the stated calories.
- **Weigh, then multiply.** If a label says 350 kcal per 100g and you eat 150g, that's 525 kcal. This is where a scale earns its keep.

## Common beginner mistakes to avoid

A few predictable traps catch almost everyone early on.

- **Forgetting the "little extras."** The oil you cook in, the splash of milk in coffee, the dressing, the handful of crisps off someone's plate. Fats and liquid calories are easy to miss and add up fast — a tablespoon of olive oil alone is around 120 calories.
- **Eyeballing calorie-dense foods.** Estimating is fine for vegetables; it's risky for nut butter, cheese, oils, and granola. Weigh the dense stuff, relax on the rest.
- **Chasing perfection over consistency.** Hitting your macros to the exact gram isn't the goal. Landing within roughly 5–10g of each target, most days, is what actually drives results. One imperfect log beats a perfect plan you abandon.
- **Ignoring protein.** Beginners obsess over cutting carbs or fat and undershoot protein — the macro that protects muscle and keeps you full. Anchor every meal with it.
- **Trusting database entries blindly.** Crowd-sourced food databases contain errors. Cross-check anything that looks suspiciously low, and prefer verified or own-brand entries when you can.

## Putting it into practice

The realistic version of macro tracking for a beginner looks like this: set your targets once, log your meals as you go for a few weeks, weigh the calorie-dense foods, and estimate the rest. Within a fortnight you'll start to *know* what 30g of protein or 500 calories looks like on a plate — which is the real prize, because that knowledge outlasts any app.

The single biggest predictor of whether someone sticks with tracking isn't precision — it's **friction**. The fewer steps between eating and logging, the longer people last. That's exactly why photo logging tools like [Nutrify](https://nutrify.info) exist: snap the meal, get your macros, move on with your day. Lower friction, more consistent data, better results.

As a light note: these targets and methods are **general guidance**, and individual needs genuinely vary with health conditions, medication, and goals. If you have specific medical or dietary concerns, it's worth checking with a GP or registered dietitian before making big changes.

## Frequently asked questions

**What are macros?**
Macros are the three macronutrients that supply energy: protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Protein and carbs provide 4 calories per gram, fat provides 9. Tracking them shows not just how much you eat, but the quality and balance of it.

**How do I calculate my macros?**
Start with your daily calorie target, set protein at roughly 1.6–2.2g per kg of bodyweight, set fat at 0.6–1g per kg, then fill the remaining calories with carbs. A calculator or tracking app will do the arithmetic for you.

**Do I have to weigh my food?**
No. Weighing is the most accurate method and great for learning portion sizes early on, but you can track perfectly well by estimating portions or using photo logging — especially when you're out. Many people weigh at home and estimate everywhere else.

**How long should I track macros for?**
There's no fixed rule. Most beginners track for a few weeks to a few months to learn portions and habits, then keep the habits and stop the daily logging. Some keep tracking long-term; both are fine.

**Is tracking calories or macros better for beginners?**
Calories tell you if you'll gain or lose weight; macros tell you how you'll look and feel doing it. Tracking macros automatically tracks calories too, so for most beginners, macros give you more useful information for the same effort.

## The bottom line

Tracking macros is simply counting the protein, carbs, and fat in what you eat — set realistic targets, log consistently rather than perfectly, weigh the calorie-dense foods, and let estimating handle the rest. The habit is far more valuable than the precision, and the goal is to learn portions well enough that you eventually won't need to track at all.
